Prompting toolkit

Prompting cheat sheet

Eight habits that turn a good prompt into a great one. Keep these in mind every time you type.

1

Set the role

Start with who Copilot should be: “You are a policy analyst for Sharjah Digital Department…”

2

Be specific about output

Say the format and length: “a one-page brief”, “5 slides”, “a table with 3 columns”.

3

Ground it on your data

Point to a file: “Using the attached SDD adoption data…” so answers are real, not generic.

4

Iterate, don’t restart

Refine in the same chat: “make it shorter”, “add a chart”, “more formal tone”.

5

Ask it to show its work

“Explain how you got that number” builds trust and catches mistakes.

6

Give a style example

Paste a sentence you like: “write it in this tone” beats describing the tone.

7

Chain, don’t cram

Break big asks into steps: research → analyse → draft → present.

8

Verify high-stakes

Always double-check names, figures, and anything citizen-facing before you share it.

Live demo station

Researcher Agent

Copilot’s deep-research mode gathers, compares, and cites sources — perfect for benchmarking Sharjah against the world’s best digital governments.

Research · compare · cite

Use this to open with a strong smart-government story

Great for showing that Copilot can research beyond a simple answer and surface patterns across multiple credible sources.

1
What to showLive source gathering, cross-country comparison, and a structured summary.
2
Why it mattersIt helps the audience see that AI can support informed policy thinking, not just writing.
3
Best moment to pauseWhen sources appear — click one to demonstrate traceability and trust.
👀 Watch for during the demo: Copilot lists its sources as it researches. Click one to show the audience that every claim traces back to a real page — then ask it to refine the summary for a Youth Council audience.
Try these variants:
  • “Add a comparison table with a column for each country.”
  • “Now focus only on ideas that a youth council could help pilot.”
  • “Rewrite the summary for a 2-minute spoken introduction.”
Benchmark smart governments
Beginner5–7 minLive demo
You are a policy researcher for Sharjah Digital Department. Research how three leading smart-government programmes — Estonia's e-Governance, Singapore's Smart Nation, and the UAE federal digital-government model — approach digital services, citizen experience, and innovation. Summarise the strongest ideas Sharjah could learn from in a concise comparison with clear headings and source-backed findings. End with 3 practical recommendations relevant to a youth council audience.
Digital sustainability angle
Intermediate4–6 minResearch brief
Research how governments measure the environmental impact of going paperless, including carbon savings and paper reduction. Summarise the 3 most common metrics, explain in simple language how each is calculated, and give one credible example of a public-sector organisation using each metric. Present the answer in a workshop-friendly format with short headings and plain-English takeaways.
Hands-on data station

Analyst Agent

Point Copilot at a spreadsheet and it reads, analyses, and charts the data for you — no formulas required.

Analyze · explain · chart

Turn spreadsheets into insights the room can understand

This section works best when you want a visible aha moment: the data is there, but Copilot turns it into a story in seconds.

1
What to showRanking, weakest performers, trends, and explanation of how conclusions were reached.
2
Audience momentAsk participants which service they think has the weakest adoption before revealing the result.
3
Best follow-upAsk for a chart or a slide-ready summary immediately after the answer appears.
✅ Expected outcome: A clear explanation of the weakest services, the long wait times, and one or two intervention ideas you can immediately discuss.
Try these variants:
  • “Rank the services from best to worst digital adoption.”
  • “Which government sector saved the most hours in 2025?”
  • “Draft a 3-bullet insight I could put on a slide.”
Find the weak spots
Beginner5 minUses adoption file
Using SharjahDigitalDepartment_Digital_Services_Adoption.xlsx, analyse the eight government services. Tell me: (1) which service has the lowest digital adoption percentage, (2) which has the longest average completion time, and (3) which service should be the top priority for improvement if Sharjah wants faster citizen uptake. Explain your reasoning in plain language and end with 3 practical recommendations.
Tell the impact story
Beginner4 minUses KPI file

Then give me 3 headline numbers I could use on a slide, and suggest the best chart type to visualise the story.
Create polished content

Word

Turn findings into a polished, government-ready document in seconds.

Draft · structure · polish

Show how raw insight becomes a proper briefing note

This is where the audience sees the jump from analysis to communication — a key workshop moment for leaders and presenters.

👀 Watch for during the demo: Copilot builds the full document with headings and structure — then you can say “shorten the background”, “make the tone warmer”, or “add Arabic headings”.

  • “Add an Arabic version of the title and headings.”
  • “Turn the recommendations into a checklist.”

Spreadsheet power-up

Excel

Ask Copilot to add formulas, spot trends, and build charts — it does the spreadsheet work for you.

Formula · format · chart

Show practical spreadsheet assistance, not just analysis

This works well after Analyst because it proves Copilot can also manipulate the sheet, calculate changes, and format outputs directly.

👀 Watch for during the demo: Copilot writes real, working formulas — not typed-in numbers — so the sheet still recalculates if the data changes.
Try these variants:
  • “Which KPI grew fastest overall, and by what percentage?”
  • “Add a total row and format all savings as AED.”
  • “Make a pie chart of the 2025 Sector Breakdown.”

Reusable workflow

Cowork Skill

Build a reusable skill once, then run it again and again with different inputs — Copilot remembers your standard format.

Standardize · reuse · scale

Show how one good workflow becomes repeatable

This section is ideal for demonstrating consistency. Once the skill exists, participants immediately understand the time-saving value.

👀 Watch for during the demo: Once the skill exists, generating the next brief takes one line. Run it again for “Traffic Fine Payment” to show how a reusable skill scales.
Try these variants:
  • “Add an Arabic summary line to the skill’s output.”
  • “Run the skill for three services and combine them into one document.”
  • “Add a 1–5 digital readiness score to each brief.”
Create the “SDD Service Brief” skill
Intermediate6 minReusable skill
Create a Cowork skill called “SDD Service Brief”. Given the name of a Sharjah government service, it should produce a standard one-page brief with these sections: Purpose, Target Users, Digital Channel, Citizen Steps, Common Pain Points, and One Improvement Idea. Write the skill so the tone is clear, practical, and suitable for workshop sharing.
Finale workflow

Cowork End-to-End

The finale: one prompt that chains research, analysis, a deck, and an email — showing how Copilot handles a whole workflow.

Chain tasks · produce outputs · decide

From prompt to decision-ready output in one flow

This is the closing moment that ties the workshop together. It shows not one feature, but an entire working pattern.

👀 Watch for during the demo: Copilot works step by step and produces several finished deliverables from a single prompt. Pause after each step so the audience can see the chain unfold.

  • “Add a step 6: three questions to ask citizens to validate the plan.”
From data to decision, in one flow
Advanced7–10 minMulti-step workflow
Act as my digital-transformation analyst for Sharjah Digital Department. Work through these steps and show me each one:
1. Review the attached adoption and KPI files.
2. Identify the two services with the weakest digital adoption.
3. Draft a short, practical improvement plan for each (2–3 actions).
4. Build a 4-slide summary deck of your findings.
5. Write a 5-line email to the Youth Council chair summarising what you found and what you recommend.
